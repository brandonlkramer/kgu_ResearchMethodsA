Chapter 9

Differences between Two Means
with T-Tests

Think-Aloud Methodology & Teaching Sarcasm

Far better an approximate answer to the right question, which is often vague, than an exact answer to the wrong question, which can always be made precise.

— Attributed to Tukey (1962)

A t-test answers a deceptively simple question: are two scores the same or different? Despite its apparent simplicity, this test is useful across a wide range of research settings. All of the statistical tests in this chapter involve comparing two mean scores measured on the same instrument.

Owing to random chance, scores from two groups on the same test are unlikely to be exactly the same — even if both sets of scores come from the same people. A t-test tells us whether the difference between two means is large enough to conclude that the groups are genuinely different, or whether scores at Time 1 differ meaningfully from scores at Time 2.

This chapter focuses on the two t-tests most commonly encountered in applied linguistics and language research:

Section 9.2–9.5
Independent Samples
t-Test

Two separate groups are compared on the same measure. Groups are independent — different people in each.

Section 9.6–9.8
Paired Samples
t-Test

The same group is measured at two time points, or on two related measures. Groups share participants.

9.1Types of t-Tests

Before running any analysis, you need to identify which type of t-test fits your data. The key question is: are your two groups independent of each other, or do they share participants?

Test When to Use Example
Independent Samples t-test Two separate groups; different participants in each Think-aloud vs. non-think-aloud group scores on a comprehension task
Paired Samples t-test Same participants measured twice, or on two related measures Pretest vs. posttest scores from the same learners
One-Sample t-test One group compared against a known or hypothetical value Comparing program GRE scores against a university-wide average of 600
⚠ Important

A t-test can only compare exactly two means at the same time. If you have three or more groups, you cannot use a t-test to test all groups simultaneously — use a one-way ANOVA instead (see Chapter 10).

9.2Independent Samples t-Test

Example Study

📖 Example: Leow & Morgan-Short (2004)

This chapter uses data from Leow and Morgan-Short (2004), who investigated whether performing a think-aloud task while completing a language activity affected learners' comprehension and production scores. Participants were assigned to one of two conditions:

Think-Aloud group — verbalized their thoughts while doing the task
Non-Think-Aloud group — completed the task silently

Because two separate groups of different people were compared, this is an ideal situation for an independent samples t-test.

9.3Assumptions of the Independent Samples t-Test

Before interpreting output, verify that your data meet the assumptions of the parametric t-test. When they are violated, consider bootstrapping or robust alternatives.

  • 1
    Independence of observations Each participant contributes only one score, and scores in one group are unrelated to scores in the other. This is guaranteed by good experimental design.
  • 2
    Normality Scores within each group should be approximately normally distributed. Check this visually with boxplots and Q-Q plots, or formally with a Shapiro-Wilk test. With large samples (n > 30), mild deviations are generally tolerable.
  • 3
    Homogeneity of variances The variance of both groups should be roughly equal. Test with Levene's test or the Brown-Forsythe test in JASP. If variances are unequal, use Welch's t-test (JASP's default).
💡 Practical Tip

Because it may seem counterintuitive: for Levene's test, you want a non-significant result (p > .05). The null hypothesis here is that the variances are equal — rejecting it means they are not. When in doubt, always use Welch's t-test (unequal variances assumed), which handles both equal and unequal variance situations safely.

9.4Running an Independent Samples t-Test in JASP

JASP Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Load the Leow & Morgan-Short data file. Confirm that RecPostScore is set to Scale and Group is set to Nominal. Data should be in long format: one row per participant.

  2. Click the T-Tests module in the top menu bar, then select Independent Samples T-Test.

  3. Move your dependent variable (RecPostScore) into the Dependent Variables box on the right.

  4. Move your grouping variable (Group) into the Grouping Variable box below.

  5. Under Tests, check ☑ Welch (recommended as the default — it handles unequal variances automatically).

  6. Under Additional Statistics, check ☑ Location parameter and ☑ Confidence interval (set to 95%).

  7. Under Additional Statistics, also check ☑ Effect size and select the appropriate standardizer. Check ☑ Confidence interval for the effect size too.

  8. Under Assumption Checks, check ☑ Normality, ☑ Equality of variances, and ☑ Q-Q plot residuals.

  9. Under Plots, check ☑ Raincloud plots to visualize data distribution in both groups.

💡 Data Format: Long vs. Wide

For an independent samples t-test, data must be in long format: one interval-level column containing scores for both groups, plus a separate categorical column indicating which group each row belongs to. Do not put Group 1 scores in one column and Group 2 scores in another (that would be wide format).

Interpreting the Confidence Interval

The 95% CI for the difference in means is [−4.37, 0.42]. Because this interval contains zero, we cannot conclude that there is a statistically significant difference between the groups. Zero is a plausible value for the true mean difference.

However, the width of the interval — nearly 5 points on a 17-point scale — tells us that our estimate is quite uncertain. We cannot rule out differences as large as about 4 points in either direction.

📊 Effect Size

The effect size using Glass' Δ (chosen because of unequal variances; uses the SD of the non-think-aloud group as standardizer) was Glass' Δ = −0.33 [−0.78, 0.13]. By Plonsky and Oswald's (2014) guidelines for SLA research, this would be considered a small effect. Note that the 95% CI for the effect size also spans zero.

9.6Paired Samples t-Test

The paired samples t-test (also called the matched samples t-test) is used when the two sets of scores you want to compare come from the same participants. Because the same people contribute data to both conditions, the groups are not independent, and the paired t-test accounts for this dependency.

📖 Example: Kim (2013)

In Kim (2013), nine learners of English as an L2 were tested on their ability to understand sarcasm. They took a pretest before instruction, an immediate posttest after nine weekly instructional sessions, and a delayed posttest five weeks later. Because the same nine participants appear at every time point, a paired samples t-test is appropriate.

Participants scored up to 30 points total (one point per correct sarcasm identification + up to 2 bonus points per item for identifying the cue).

9.7Running a Paired Samples t-Test in JASP

JASP Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Load the Kim (2013) data. Confirm that Pretest, Posttest, and DelayedPost are all set to Scale.

  2. Click the T-Tests module, then select Paired Samples T-Test.

  3. Pair your variables: click Pretest from the variable list, then Posttest. Both move together into the right panel as Pair 1. Repeat for Posttest & DelayedPost as Pair 2.

  4. Under Tests, keep the default Student test checked (the classic parametric version).

  5. Under Additional Statistics, check ☑ Location parameter and ☑ Confidence interval (95%).

  6. Under Additional Statistics, check ☑ Effect size. Select Cohen's dᵣₘ — this is recommended because it does not overestimate effects unlike other within-group d family members.

  7. Under Assumption Checks, check ☑ Normality to run a Shapiro-Wilk test on the difference scores.

  8. Under Plots, check ☑ Raincloud plots to examine the full distribution.

Interpreting the Results

For the pretest–posttest comparison, the 95% CI is [−14.37, −3.85]. Because this interval does not contain zero — and is entirely negative — we conclude there is a statistically significant improvement from pretest to posttest. Scores improved by at least about 4 points, and possibly as much as 14 points.

For the posttest–delayed posttest comparison, the 95% CI is [−5.20, −1.03], also entirely below zero, indicating continued improvement (or retention) was maintained through the delayed test.

📊 Effect Size

For the pretest–posttest comparison, Cohen's drm = 2.18 [0.21, 4.14]. This indicates that the difference between the pretest and posttest was approximately twice the standard deviation of the difference scores — a very large effect by any conventional standard. However, the wide confidence interval (spanning from 0.21 to 4.14) reflects the small sample size (N = 9) and urges caution in over-interpreting the point estimate.

⚠ Small Sample Caution

With only nine participants, confidence intervals will be wide and effect size estimates unstable. The Kim (2013) results are promising, but replication with a larger sample is essential before drawing strong conclusions.

9.9Writing Up Your Results

A complete write-up should address: (1) whether assumptions were met; (2) descriptive statistics for each group; (3) the CI for the mean difference; (4) effect size with its CI; and (5) a plain-language interpretation. A t-value, df, and p-value are optional if the CI is present.

Independent Samples t-Test — Example Write-Up

Model Reporting Paragraph

Data from Leow and Morgan-Short (2004) showed departures from normality in that there were outliers, distributions were not symmetric, and variances were not equal. An independent samples t-test, 95% CI [−4.37, 0.42], found no evidence of a difference between scores on the receptive posttest task for the think-aloud group (M = 5.89, SD = 6.04, n = 38) and the non-think-aloud group (M = 3.92, SD = 4.31, n = 39).

Since the 95% CI contains zero, we conclude that there is no statistical difference between groups. The wide CI, nearly 5 points on a test where the maximum score was 17, shows that the estimate of how different the groups are is uncertain, but not likely to be far from zero. The effect size using Glass' Δ (1976), calculated with the SD of the non-think-aloud group to account for unequal variances, was Glass' Δ = −0.33 [−0.78, 0.13] — a small effect by Plonsky and Oswald's (2014) guidelines for SLA research.

Paired Samples t-Test — Example Write-Up

Model Reporting Paragraph

Data for the pretest and posttest showed some departures from normality in having a skewed distribution. Standard deviations showed that scores at the two testing times did not have homogeneous variance, with less variance on the immediate and delayed posttests (pretest: M = 7.44, SD = 6.24, N = 9; posttest: M = 16.56, SD = 2.96, N = 9).

A parametric paired samples t-test found that a 95% CI for the difference between the pretest and posttest showed a statistically significant and practically important difference [−14.37, −3.85]. Because this CI does not cross zero, the difference between groups is statistically significant. The Cohen's drm effect size was drm = 2.18 [0.21, 4.14], meaning that the pretest-to-posttest difference was approximately twice the SD of the difference scores — a very large effect, indicating that the sarcasm instruction was highly effective. However, the wide CI reflects the small sample size and suggests that the experiment should be repeated with more participants to obtain a more precise estimate.

9.10Summary

Independent Samples Paired Samples
Design Two separate groups (different people) Same people at two times or on two measures
Key assumption Observations are independent across groups Observations are dependent (paired) within groups
Variance check Levene's / Brown-Forsythe; use Welch if unequal Check variance of difference scores
Effect size Cohen's ds, Hedges' g, or Glass' Δ Cohen's drm (recommended)
Example Leow & Morgan-Short (2004): think-aloud vs. not Kim (2013): sarcasm pretest vs. posttest

t-tests are among the most frequently used tools in applied linguistics research, but interpreting them well requires going beyond the p-value. Always report confidence intervals for the mean difference and effect sizes with their own CIs to give readers a full picture of what your data show.
