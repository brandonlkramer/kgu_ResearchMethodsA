Two-Way Factorial ANOVA in JASP

Step-by-step instructions with annotated screenshots

← Concept & Theory JASP Walkthrough (this page)

This page walks through a 2 × 2 × 2 factorial ANOVA in JASP using the Obarow (2004) dataset. We test the effects of Music, Illustrations, and Gender on vocabulary gain scores. Each step is paired with a screenshot slot — click any dashed box to upload. Red callouts show what to look at; blue callouts explain the output; yellow callouts flag common mistakes.

📂 Sample Data

File: Obarow.Story1.csv
Variables: PicturesT1 (Nominal — pictures/no pictures), MusicT1 (Nominal — music/no music), gender (Nominal — male/female), gnsc1.1 (Scale — vocabulary gain score).
Confirm variable types before starting: IVs = Nominal, DV = Scale.

12A.1Setting Up the Analysis

  1. 1

    In the top menu bar, click the ANOVA module.

  2. 2

    Select ANOVA from the Classical section of the dropdown (the same option used for one-way ANOVA in Chapter 10).

  3. 3

    Move gnsc1.1 (the vocabulary gain score) into the Dependent Variable box.

  4. 4

    Move PicturesT1, MusicT1, and gender into the Fixed Factors box. For reproducibility, add them in this order: Pictures first, then Music, then Gender.

  5. 5

    Check ☑ Descriptive statistics and ☑ Estimates of effect size. Select ηp² (partial eta-squared) with ☑ Confidence interval.

①

Dependent Variable: gnsc1.1 (gain score) appears here.

Fixed Factors: All three IVs — PicturesT1, MusicT1, gender — appear here. JASP will automatically model all main effects and all interactions among these variables.

12A.2Model Menu: Changing to Type II Sum of Squares

JASP defaults to Type III Sum of Squares. For factorial ANOVA in most applied linguistics contexts, Type II SS is preferred. This must be changed manually.

  1. 6

    Click the Model section to expand it.

  2. 7

    Under Sum of Squares, change the dropdown from Type III to Type II.

Fig 12–2Model menu — Sum of Squares changed to Type II
screenshots/fig12-2.png
Model menu open: Sum of Squares dropdown showing "Type II" selected
!

This step is easy to forget. Always check the Model menu before running. Type III SS gives different (and often misleading) results when group sizes are unequal, which they almost always are in real SLA data. Type II SS is more robust and appropriate for most research designs.

12A.3Selecting Options & Plots

  1. 8

    Open the Assumption Checks section. Check ☑ Homogeneity tests (Levene's test) and ☑ Q-Q plot of residuals.

  2. 9

    Open the Descriptives Plots section. This is where you set up your interaction plot. Move one variable to Horizontal axis, one to Separate lines, and (optionally) one to Separate plots. A common arrangement for the Obarow data: PicturesT1 on the horizontal axis, MusicT1 as separate lines, and gender as separate plots.

JASP will produce one interaction plot per level of your Separate plots variable (here, one plot for males and one for females). Each plot shows how the effect of Pictures (x-axis) differs for Music vs. No Music conditions (separate lines).

12A.4Output: Descriptive Statistics

The descriptives table shows the mean, SD, and N for every combination of your IVs. With three 2-level IVs, there are 8 cells (2 × 2 × 2).

Fig 12–4Descriptive Statistics table — means and SDs for all 8 cells
screenshots/fig12-4.png
Descriptives: 8 rows (all Music×Pictures×Gender combinations), N, Mean, SD for each
①

Note the small n in each cell — some cells have as few as 5 participants. This is a design weakness: with such small cell sizes, power is low and effect size estimates will be imprecise. Report cell sizes in your write-up to alert readers.

Also compare the overall male vs. female means: Males M = 1.53 (SD = 1.76) vs. Females M = 0.67 (SD = 1.12). This main effect of Gender will appear in the ANOVA table.

12A.5Output: Interaction Plot

The interaction plot is the most important visual in a factorial ANOVA. Study it before looking at the ANOVA table — it tells you immediately whether interactions are likely.

Fig 12–5Interaction plot — Music × Pictures, separate panels for Gender
screenshots/fig12-5.png
Two panels (Male / Female): x-axis = Pictures condition; separate lines = Music condition
→

How to read this plot: If lines within a panel are roughly parallel, there is no Music × Pictures interaction for that gender. If they cross or strongly diverge, an interaction is present.

Comparing the two panels (Male vs. Female): If the pattern of lines differs between panels, a three-way interaction (Music × Pictures × Gender) may be present.

For the Obarow data: Males show a pattern where music helps more without pictures than with pictures, while females show the opposite tendency — but neither effect was strong enough to reach statistical significance given the small sample sizes.

12A.6Output: Assumption Checks

Fig 12–6Levene's Test for Homogeneity of Variances
screenshots/fig12-6.png
Levene's test table: F, df1, df2, p — result should be non-significant (p = .090)
①

Levene's test result: F(7, 56) = 1.88, p = .090. Because p > .05, we do not reject the null hypothesis of equal variances. The homogeneity assumption is met — the parametric factorial ANOVA is appropriate.

If Levene's test were significant, consider bootstrapping (available in JASP's Post Hoc Tests menu) to obtain more robust CIs.

Fig 12–7Q-Q Plot of Residuals
screenshots/fig12-7.png
Q-Q plot: residuals plotted against theoretical normal quantiles — points near diagonal = OK
→

Residuals should fall approximately along the diagonal line. Minor deviations at the tails are common even with normal data. A clear S-shape or J-shape pattern would indicate non-normality. For the Obarow data, the Q-Q plot shows no serious problems.

12A.7Output: Between Subjects Effects Table

This is the main ANOVA results table. It contains one row per main effect, one per interaction, and one Residuals row.

Fig 12–8Between Subjects Effects table — main effects and interactions
screenshots/fig12-8.png
Between Subjects Effects: rows for Pictures, Music, Gender, all 2-way interactions, 3-way interaction, Residuals
①

Read from the bottom interaction upward:

• Pictures × Music × Gender (three-way): F(1, 56) = 0.60, p = .443 — not significant.

• Two-way interactions (Pictures × Music; Pictures × Gender; Music × Gender): all p > .05 — none significant.

• Main effects: Only Gender is significant, F(1, 56) = 4.35, p = .042, ηp² = .072. Pictures and Music are not significant.

②

The Residuals row (SS = 128.60, df = 56) represents the unexplained within-cell variance — the denominator for all F-ratios. A large Residuals SS relative to the other rows confirms that the model explains relatively little variance (R² ≈ .148).

💡 Reading Order Reminder

  1. Check the highest-order interaction first (three-way for this design). If significant, interpret it — main effects become secondary.

  2. If three-way is not significant, check each two-way interaction. A significant two-way means you must interpret the main effects of those two variables together.

  3. If no interactions are significant, interpret main effects independently. For significant main effects with only 2 levels, compare descriptive means directly — no post-hoc test needed.

12A.8Post-Hoc Tests

For the Obarow data, the only significant effect is the main effect of Gender — which has only two levels (male/female). Because there are only two groups, you already know from the descriptive statistics which group scored higher. No post-hoc test is needed for a significant 2-level main effect.

📌 When You Do Need Post-Hoc Tests

Post-hoc tests are needed when a significant effect has 3 or more levels, or when a significant interaction requires follow-up. For interactions, use JASP's Conditional comparisons option (in the Post Hoc Tests menu) — this compares levels of one variable at each level of another, properly accounting for the interaction structure.

Setting Up Post-Hoc Tests (for practice or when needed)

  1. 10

    Open the Post Hoc Tests section.

  2. 11

    Move the variable(s) you want comparisons for into the right panel. For a significant interaction, move the interaction term (e.g., MusicT1 × gender), not just individual variables.

  3. 12

    Check ☑ Effect size (Cohen's dₛ) and ☑ Confidence intervals. For interactions, also check ☑ Conditional comparisons for interactions.

  4. 13

    For bootstrapped CIs: click From … Bootstraps and set to 10,000 samples, CI type BCa. Especially useful when Levene's test is violated.

Fig 12–9Post Hoc Tests setup — interaction term selected, conditional comparisons enabled
screenshots/fig12-9.png
Post Hoc panel: interaction term in right box, Effect size + CI + Conditional comparisons checked
→

When Conditional comparisons for interactions is checked, JASP compares groups within each level of the conditioning variable. For example, for a Music × Gender interaction, it compares males vs. females separately for the Music and No Music conditions — allowing you to see where the interaction is driven from.

Fig 12–10Post Hoc output — pairwise comparisons with CIs and effect sizes
screenshots/fig12-10.png
Post Hoc table: Mean Diff, SE, t, p, 95% CI, Cohen's dₛ for each comparison
①

Read the 95% CI column for each comparison. CIs that do not contain zero indicate a statistically significant difference. The Cohen's dₛ column shows the standardized effect size for that specific pairwise comparison.

Wide CIs (as expected with small cell sizes here) indicate uncertainty — the true effect could be much larger or smaller than the point estimate.

12A.9Model Write-Up

Model Reporting Paragraph — Two-Way (+ Gender) Factorial ANOVA

A 2 (Music: music vs. no music) × 2 (Illustrations: pictures vs. no pictures) × 2 (Gender: male vs. female) between-subjects factorial ANOVA was conducted on immediate vocabulary gain scores using Type II Sum of Squares. Descriptive statistics showed that males (M = 1.53, SD = 1.76, n = 34) outperformed females (M = 0.67, SD = 1.12, n = 30) overall. Levene's test for homogeneity of variances was not significant, F(7, 56) = 1.88, p = .090, indicating that the equal-variances assumption was met. The Q-Q plot of residuals showed no serious departure from normality.

Starting from the highest-order interaction: the three-way Music × Illustrations × Gender interaction was not statistically significant, F(1, 56) = 0.60, p = .443, ηp² = .011. None of the two-way interactions reached significance: Music × Illustrations, F(1, 56) = 0.73, p = .398, ηp² = .013; Music × Gender, F(1, 56) = 2.42, p = .126, ηp² = .041; Illustrations × Gender, F(1, 56) = 0.12, p = .733, ηp² = .002. Among the main effects, Gender was the only significant predictor, F(1, 56) = 4.35, p = .042, ηp² = .072. Because Gender has only two levels and no significant interaction qualified this effect, the direction of the effect is evident from the descriptive statistics: males gained approximately one more vocabulary word than females across all conditions. Neither Music, F(1, 56) = 1.04, p = .312, nor Illustrations, F(1, 56) = 0.16, p = .692, produced significant main effects. The overall model accounted for approximately 15% of the variance in gain scores (R² = .148), though the adjusted estimate was considerably smaller (R² = .042), reflecting low power due to small cell sizes.

💡 Getting R² from JASP

JASP's ANOVA module does not directly report overall R². To obtain it, open the Regression module, choose Linear Regression, put gnsc1.1 as the DV, and move all three IVs into the Factors box. Open the Model menu and move all variables (plus their interactions) into Model 1. The output will include R² and adjusted R².
