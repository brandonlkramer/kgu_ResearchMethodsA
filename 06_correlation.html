Chapter 6

Correlation

Age of Arrival, Accent, & the Shape of a Relationship

Concept & Theory (this page) JASP Walkthrough →

Readers should get used to understanding the importance of the size of the r value, and understand the size of a correlation without worrying about whether the p-value is lower than .05 or not.

A correlation measures the degree to which two continuous variables vary together. When one variable increases, does the other tend to increase too? Or decrease? Or neither? Correlation is one of the most fundamental tools in applied linguistics research — and one of the most frequently misused.

Unlike the ANOVA and t-test chapters, which focus on comparing groups, correlation is used when both variables are continuous (interval or ratio level). There are no groups, no DVs or IVs in the traditional sense — just two measurements per participant.

6.1What Is Correlation?

A correlation coefficient expresses two things simultaneously: the direction of a relationship (positive or negative) and its strength (from 0 = no relationship to ±1 = perfect relationship).

Positive Correlationr ≈ +0.95 — as X rises, Y rises
Negative Correlationr ≈ −0.95 — as X rises, Y falls
No Correlationr ≈ 0.00 — X and Y are unrelated

6.2Scatterplots First — Always

Before computing any correlation coefficient, examine a scatterplot. This is not optional. A scatterplot tells you:

  • Whether the relationship is linear — if it isn't, Pearson's r will mislead you
  • Whether there are outliers that are disproportionately pulling the line
  • Whether there are meaningful subgroups driving an apparent correlation
  • The approximate direction and strength of the relationship

A good scatterplot includes both a regression line (a straight best-fit line) and a Loess line (a flexible smooth curve). When the two lines are close, the linearity assumption is supported. When they diverge, the relationship may be non-linear and a simple Pearson correlation may not be the best summary.

📖 Example: Flege et al. (1999) — Age of Arrival & Pronunciation

Flege, Yeni-Komshian, and Liu (1999) examined 240 Korean immigrants to the USA. Their age of arrival (AOA) in years was correlated with their accent rating — a score from 1 (very foreign-accented) to 9 (native-like) given by native English speaker judges. The prediction: learners who arrived younger would sound more native-like.

A scatterplot of this data shows a clear negative linear trend: the older the learner's arrival age, the lower the pronunciation rating. The regression and Loess lines track closely, supporting a roughly linear relationship — though Flege et al. themselves fit a cubic curve to capture the slight non-linearity at younger ages.

📖 Example: DeKeyser (2000) — Age of Arrival & Grammar

DeKeyser (2000) gave 57 Hungarian immigrants to the USA a 200-point grammaticality judgment test (GJT) of English. Again, age of arrival was negatively correlated with test performance — older arrivals scored lower on the grammar test. This is a smaller, complementary dataset that demonstrates the same underlying pattern.

6.3The Correlation Coefficient r

The most commonly used correlation statistic is Pearson's r (also written as the product-moment correlation). It ranges from −1.00 (perfect negative relationship) to +1.00 (perfect positive relationship), with 0 indicating no linear relationship.

The Pearson r Scale
−1.00 −0.60 −0.25 0.00 +0.25 +0.60 +1.00
Perfect negative ← Large · Medium · Small → Perfect positive
Cohen's (1988) — General Small: r = .10 · Medium: r = .30 · Large: r = .50
Plonsky & Oswald (2014) — SLA Small: r = .25 · Medium: r = .40 · Large: r = .60

For the Flege et al. (1999) data, the Pearson correlation between AOA and pronunciation rating is r = −.86. By any benchmark, this is a very large effect — a strong, negative relationship. The negative sign tells us that higher AOA goes together with lower pronunciation scores.

6.4r² — The Variance Explained

Squaring the correlation coefficient gives r², which tells you the proportion of variance in one variable that is explained by its linear relationship with the other. This is directly interpretable as a percentage.

rr²InterpretationExample
−.86.7474% of variance in pronunciation explained by AOAFlege et al. (1999)
.18.033% of variance in GJT scores explained by hours of studyLarson-Hall (2008)
.08.006<1% of variance explained — essentially no relationshipGJT × Aptitude
📌 r² and the "New Statistics"

Because r is already an effect size, and r² is directly interpretable as a proportion, many researchers report r and r² without a p-value. A p-value tells you almost nothing useful about a correlation: with a large enough sample, any r will be "significant"; with a small sample, even a large r will not reach significance. Focus on r, r², and their 95% CIs instead.

6.5Correlation Does Not Equal Causation

This is the most important caution in all of statistics. A correlation tells you that two variables co-vary — not that one causes the other. If AOA and pronunciation are negatively correlated, there are (at least) three possible explanations:

ScenarioExplanation
A causes BArriving later causes lower pronunciation (younger brains are more plastic)
B causes AUnlikely here, but must always be considered
C causes both A and BA third variable (e.g., amount of L1 use, social networks) causes both older arrival and lower pronunciation

Only a true experiment — where the researcher manipulates the independent variable — can establish causality. Correlational studies can suggest causality through theory and logic, but cannot prove it statistically.

6.6Assumptions of Pearson's r

  • 1
    LinearityThe relationship between the two variables should follow a straight line. Check with a scatterplot showing both a regression line and a Loess smoother. If the Loess line curves substantially, consider a non-linear approach.
  • 2
    Independence of observationsEach participant contributes one pair of scores, and scores are not influenced by other participants' scores. Ensured by good research design.
  • 3
    Normal distributionBoth variables should be approximately normally distributed. Check with histograms or Q-Q plots. Moderate violations are tolerable with larger samples.
  • 4
    HomoscedasticityThe variance of one variable should be roughly equal across all values of the other. On a scatterplot, this means the spread of points around the regression line should be similar throughout the range of X.
⚠ When Assumptions Are Violated

If normality or homoscedasticity are violated, consider:
• Bootstrapped Pearson's r — randomly resamples the data to produce robust CIs without assuming normality
• Spearman's ρ — a rank-based non-parametric alternative (less preferred in modern practice)
• Percentage bend correlation or Winsorized correlation — robust alternatives available in R

6.7Types of Correlation

TestWhen to UseNotes
Pearson's r Both variables are continuous and the relationship is linear; assumptions met The standard parametric choice. Also used to report bootstrapped correlation results.
Spearman's ρ Ordinal data, or serious normality violations Converts scores to ranks before correlating. Traditional but less powerful than modern robust alternatives.
Kendall's τ-b Small samples with many tied ranks Another rank-based option; rarely used in SLA research.
Point-biserial One continuous variable, one dichotomous variable (e.g., correct/incorrect on a test item) Used in test item analysis (item discrimination). Mathematically equivalent to Pearson's r with a 0/1 variable.
Bootstrapped Pearson's r When parametric assumptions are violated Preferred over Spearman's ρ in modern practice. Available in SPSS; not yet available in JASP (as of writing).

6.8Effect Size & Benchmarks

The correlation coefficient r is itself an effect size — unlike ANOVA where you need a separate calculation. For applied linguistics and SLA research, use Plonsky and Oswald's (2014) benchmarks rather than Cohen's more general guidelines, as real SLA effects tend to be larger than those in experimental psychology.

MagnitudePlonsky & Oswald (2014) — SLACohen (1988) — General
Smallr ≥ .25r ≥ .10
Mediumr ≥ .40r ≥ .30
Larger ≥ .60r ≥ .50

6.9Confidence Intervals for r

Just as with mean differences, a 95% CI for r tells you the range of values within which the true population correlation is likely to fall. JASP calculates these automatically using Fisher's z-transformation, which converts the asymmetric distribution of r into a symmetric normal distribution before computing the CI, then transforms back.

Pearson Correlation — Flege et al. (1999): AOA × Pronunciation (JASP Output)
Variablesr95% CI Lower95% CI UpperN
AOA – PronEng−.86−.89−.82240

The CI of [−.89, −.82] is narrow because the sample is large (N = 240). It tells us that the true correlation is almost certainly between −.82 and −.89 — a large negative effect by any standard, explaining between 67% and 79% of the variance in pronunciation ratings.

💡 Wide CIs Signal Uncertainty

Small samples produce wide CIs. A correlation of r = .50 with a CI of [−.10, .85] tells you almost nothing — zero is within the plausible range. Always report the CI alongside r; without it, the reader cannot judge how precisely the true effect has been estimated.

6.10Correlation Matrices

When you have more than two variables and want to see all pairwise correlations simultaneously, a correlation matrix (also called a correlation table) is the most efficient format. Each cell shows the r between the row variable and the column variable.

A scatterplot matrix extends this to show all pairwise scatterplots simultaneously — a very efficient way to visually check for linearity and outliers across many variables at once. JASP's Correlation module produces these automatically.

📌 JASP Note

Correlation in JASP is found under the Regression module (not a standalone Correlation module). Select Regression → Correlation and move any number of variables into the Variables box. JASP will compute all pairwise correlations simultaneously and display them in a table with optional CIs.

6.11Writing Up Results

A complete report of a correlation should include: the name of the test, the r value, the 95% CI, the sample size, and r². No p-value is required if the CI is present.

Model Reporting Paragraph — Single Correlation

A Pearson correlation was conducted to examine the relationship between age of arrival (AOA) in the USA and pronunciation ratings in English among 240 Korean immigrants (Flege et al., 1999). There was a large, negative relationship between the two variables: r = −.86, 95% CI [−.89, −.82], N = 240, r² = .74. This indicates that AOA accounts for approximately 74% of the variance in English pronunciation ratings — learners who arrived younger tended to receive substantially higher (more native-like) ratings than those who arrived later. Because the sample size is large and the CI is narrow, this is a precise estimate of the true population relationship.

Model Reporting Paragraph — Multiple Correlations (Prose)

Pearson correlations were examined between scores on the grammaticality judgment test (GJT) and two potential predictors: hours of English study and language aptitude scores (Larson-Hall, 2008; N = 200). Hours of study showed a small positive relationship with GJT scores, r = .18, 95% CI [.05, .32], r² = .03 — English study time accounts for only about 3% of the variance in grammar scores. No meaningful relationship was found between aptitude and GJT scores, r = .08, 95% CI [−.06, .22], r² < .01. Both CIs indicate considerable uncertainty, and the aptitude CI spans zero, meaning the true relationship could be negligible.
