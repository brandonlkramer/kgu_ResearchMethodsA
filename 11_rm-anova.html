Chapter 11

One-Way ANOVA
(Within-Subjects / Repeated Measures)

Teaching Sarcasm & the Problem of Sphericity

Concept & Theory (this page) JASP Walkthrough →

To put it succinctly, subjects differ. When subjects are observed only once, these subject differences contribute to the error term. When subjects are observed repeatedly, we can obtain an estimate of the degree of subject differences and partial these differences out of the error term.

— Howell (2002, p. 480)

A repeated-measures ANOVA (RM-ANOVA) extends the one-way between-subjects ANOVA to situations where the same participants contribute scores at every level of the independent variable. Rather than comparing independent groups, you are comparing the same group at three or more time points — or under three or more conditions.

This chapter focuses on the one-way RM-ANOVA: one within-subjects IV with three or more levels, and one continuous DV. (When both a within-subjects and between-subjects IV are present, the design is called a mixed or split-plot ANOVA, covered in Chapter 13.)

11.1When to Use a Repeated-Measures ANOVA

Choose a one-way RM-ANOVA when the same participants appear in every condition of your categorical independent variable.

Use this when
Same People,
3+ Conditions

The same participants are measured at three or more time points, or under three or more conditions. A within-subjects IV has at least 3 levels.

Don't use if
Only 2 Levels

With only two levels (e.g., pretest vs. posttest), use a paired samples t-test instead (see Ch. 9). RM-ANOVA is needed for 3 or more levels.

Don't use if
Different People
in Each Group

When different participants appear in each group, use a between-subjects ANOVA (Ch. 10). RM-ANOVA assumes within-person correlation.

Extends from
Paired t-Test

Just as paired t-tests handle two repeated measures, RM-ANOVA handles three or more without inflating the Type I error rate.

11.2Why Repeated Measures Increases Power

The key advantage of RM-ANOVA over a standard between-subjects ANOVA is that it can factor out individual differences from the error term. Because the same person contributes scores at every level, we can estimate and remove the variance that comes simply from people being different from each other — leaving a smaller, cleaner error term.

How Variance Is Partitioned
Between-Subjects ANOVA (Ch. 10) Total variance is split into:

• Between-group variance (treatment effect)
• Within-group variance (error — includes all individual differences)
→
Repeated-Measures ANOVA (Ch. 11) Total variance is split into:

• Between-condition variance (treatment effect)
• Subject variance (individual differences — factored out)
• Residual error (smaller — more power)
A smaller error term means the F-ratio is larger for the same treatment effect → greater statistical power → easier to detect real differences.

11.3Example Study: Kim (2013)

📖 Example: Kim (2013)

Kim (2013) investigated whether concept-based instruction using Schema for the Orienting Basis of Action (SCOBAs) could help L1 Korean learners of English better recognize sarcasm. Nine participants completed a sarcasm comprehension test — worth up to 30 points — at three time points:

Pretest — before any instruction
Posttest — one week after completing nine weekly instructional sessions
Delayed Posttest — five weeks after the posttest

Because the same nine participants took all three tests, this is a repeated-measures design. With three time points (3 levels), it calls for a one-way RM-ANOVA rather than multiple paired t-tests.

11.5Assumptions & Sphericity

The RM-ANOVA shares the standard parametric assumptions of normality and homogeneity of variances, but adds a third assumption that is unique to repeated-measures designs: sphericity.

  • 1
    Normality of residuals The residuals (differences between observed scores and fitted values) should be approximately normally distributed. Check with a Q-Q plot in JASP. RM-ANOVA is moderately robust to violations with larger samples.
  • 2
    Homogeneity of variances Variance of scores should be similar across the levels of the within-subjects IV. Examine side-by-side boxplots. Note that unequal variance in repeated-measures data is related to the sphericity assumption below.
  • ★
    Sphericity The variances of the differences between all pairs of conditions should be equal. This is the critical and unique assumption of RM-ANOVA — explained in detail below.

What Is Sphericity?

Sphericity requires that the variance of the difference scores between each pair of time points is equal across all pairs. For example, with three time points (Pretest, Posttest, Delayed):

Pair of ConditionsDifference ScoresVariance of Differences
Pretest − PosttestFor each person: their pretest score minus their posttest scoreShould be ≈ equal to others
Pretest − DelayedFor each person: pretest minus delayed posttest scoreShould be ≈ equal to others
Posttest − DelayedFor each person: posttest minus delayed posttest scoreShould be ≈ equal to others

When these variances are not equal, the F-test is too liberal — it produces p-values that are too small and inflates the Type I error rate. The solution is to apply a correction factor to the degrees of freedom.

✅
Sphericity Met

Mauchly's p > .05. Variances of difference scores are approximately equal. The uncorrected F-test is valid.

⚠️
Sphericity Violated

Mauchly's p < .05. Difference score variances are unequal. The uncorrected F-test is too liberal. A correction is needed.

🔧
Always Correct Anyway

Howell (2002) recommends applying a correction factor regardless of Mauchly's result, since the test itself is unreliable (low power with small samples).

11.6Sphericity Corrections

Both the Greenhouse–Geisser (GG) and Huynh–Feldt (HF) corrections work by multiplying the degrees of freedom by a correction factor called epsilon (ε). This reduces the effective df and raises the p-value, making the test more conservative.

CorrectionWhen to UseNotes
None (Sphericity assumed) Only if sphericity is clearly met Most liberal; produces smallest p-values
Greenhouse–Geisser (GG) When ε < .75, or as a conservative default More conservative than HF; ε can range from 1/(k−1) to 1.00
Huynh–Feldt (HF) When ε > .75 (Field, 2018); or as default (Abdi, 2010) Less conservative than GG; can slightly exceed 1.00 (capped at 1.00 in JASP)
💡 Practical Recommendation

Always report a corrected result. A common approach is to use the Greenhouse–Geisser correction by default (when ε < .75) and Huynh–Feldt when ε > .75. Howell (2002) recommends applying a correction even when Mauchly's test is not significant, since the test has low power in small samples. In practice, the correction rarely changes the conclusion — but always report which correction was used and what ε was.

📌 How to Read the Table

The df column is where corrections are visible. Without correction, df = k−1 = 2 for Time (three conditions) and df = (N−1)(k−1) = 16 for Residuals. After applying GG or HF corrections, these are multiplied by ε — reducing them and making the test more conservative. The F-value itself does not change; only the df (and therefore the p-value) changes. Always report the corrected df alongside the F value: e.g., F(1.35, 10.79) = …

11.8Post-Hoc Tests

Just as with between-subjects ANOVA, a significant omnibus F-test tells you only that some difference exists across time points — not which specific pairs differ. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons are needed to identify where the differences lie.

In JASP, open the Post Hoc Tests menu and move your within-subjects IV to the right panel. JASP will compare all pairs of levels. Key options to select:

OptionWhat It DoesRecommendation
Effect sizeAdds Cohen's dₛ (standardized mean difference between each pair)Always check this
Confidence intervals95% CI for the mean difference and for the effect sizeAlways check this
Correction (e.g., Bonferroni)Adjusts p-values for multiple comparisonsUse Bonferroni if using p-values; CI approach does not require correction

For the Kim (2013) data, we would compare all three pairs: Pretest–Posttest, Pretest–Delayed, and Posttest–Delayed. We already know from the paired t-test analyses (Ch. 9) that both the Pretest–Posttest and Posttest–Delayed comparisons were significant. The RM-ANOVA post-hoc tests will confirm these findings while controlling the error rate.

11.9Effect Sizes

Effect SizeUsed ForInterpretation
Partial η² (ηp²) Omnibus within-subjects effect Proportion of variance explained by the IV, after removing variance due to individual differences. Reported directly in JASP output. Small ≈ .01, Medium ≈ .06, Large ≈ .14 (Cohen's guidelines).
Cohen's dₛ Each pairwise post-hoc comparison Standardized mean difference between two specific conditions. Calculated the same way as for between-subjects t-tests using the pooled SD. By Plonsky & Oswald: 0.60 = small, 1.00 = medium, 1.50 = large.
Cohen's dᵣₘ Within-group pairwise comparisons Accounts for the correlation between repeated measures. Appropriate when reporting individual paired comparisons from a repeated-measures design. Available via JASP paired-samples t-test output.

11.10Writing Up Results

A complete write-up should include: data format, assumption check results (normality, sphericity and ε), the corrected omnibus F-test, post-hoc pairwise comparisons with CIs and effect sizes, and a plain-language interpretation.

Model Reporting Paragraph — One-Way RM-ANOVA

To examine whether sarcasm comprehension improved across three testing times, a one-way repeated-measures ANOVA was conducted with Time (Pretest, Posttest, Delayed Posttest) as the within-subjects independent variable and sarcasm test score as the dependent variable. Descriptive statistics were: Pretest, M = 7.44, SD = 5.20; Posttest, M = 16.56, SD = 2.96; Delayed Posttest, M = 19.67, SD = 2.96; all N = 9. Data distributions showed some departure from normality (positively skewed at Pretest and Delayed Posttest). Mauchly's test of sphericity [reported from output], and the Greenhouse–Geisser correction was applied (ε = [reported from output]).

The omnibus within-subjects effect of Time was statistically significant, F([corrected df]) = [F-value], p [p-value], ηp² = [value], indicating that scores changed significantly across the three testing times. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons with Bonferroni correction revealed that scores improved significantly from Pretest to Posttest (mean difference = −9.11, 95% CI [−14.37, −3.85], Cohen's dᵣₘ = 2.18), and from Posttest to Delayed Posttest (mean difference = −3.11, 95% CI [−5.20, −1.03], Cohen's dᵣₘ = 1.04). Both comparisons showed large and medium effect sizes respectively. These results indicate that concept-based instruction was effective in improving sarcasm comprehension, and that gains were maintained and even extended five weeks after instruction ended.
