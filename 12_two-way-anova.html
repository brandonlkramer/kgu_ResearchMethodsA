Chapter 12

Two-Way Factorial ANOVA

Music, Pictures, Gender & the Power of Interaction

Concept & Theory (this page) JASP Walkthrough →

One important reason for doing a factorial ANOVA is to uncover possible interactions among the variables. This result could not be uncovered in separate t-tests or separate one-way ANOVAs.

A two-way factorial ANOVA tests the effects of two categorical independent variables on one continuous dependent variable — simultaneously. The "two-way" refers to having two IVs; the "factorial" means we model not only each variable's individual effect (main effects) but also whether the two variables interact with each other (interaction effect).

This is the key advantage over running two separate one-way ANOVAs: factorial ANOVA can detect when the effect of one variable depends on the level of another. Interactions are often the most interesting findings in a study.

12.1When to Use a Two-Way Factorial ANOVA

RequirementDetails
2 independent variablesBoth must be categorical (nominal). Each IV must have 2 or more levels.
1 dependent variableMust be continuous (interval or ratio scale).
Between-subjects designEach participant appears in only one combination of the two IVs. If any IV is within-subjects, use a mixed or repeated-measures ANOVA instead.
Independent observationsEach participant contributes one score; groups do not overlap.
⚠ ANOVA Mindset Syndrome

A common mistake is to artificially split a continuous variable into groups just to run a factorial ANOVA. For example, converting TOEFL scores into "Low/Medium/High" proficiency groups discards valuable information. If a variable is naturally continuous, regression is a better choice. Use factorial ANOVA only when your IVs are genuinely categorical.

12.2Design Notation & Structure

A two-way factorial ANOVA is described using the notation a × b, where a and b are the number of levels in each IV. For example, a 2 × 2 ANOVA has two IVs each with two levels, creating four unique cells.

📖 Example: Obarow (2004)

Obarow (2004) investigated whether the presentation format of a storybook affected incidental vocabulary learning in young L2 learners (kindergarten and Grade 1). The two IVs were:

Music (2 levels): Story was sung with music · Story was read without music
Illustrations (2 levels): Large picture book provided · No pictures given

The DV was a vocabulary gain score (posttest minus pretest score) on words encountered in the story. For illustration purposes, a third IV of Gender (male/female) was also included, making it a 2 × 2 × 2 design. This chapter introduces the logic using the two-way Music × Illustrations design.

12.3Main Effects vs. Interaction

A factorial ANOVA tests three things simultaneously:

  1. Main effect of IV1 (Music): Does adding music affect vocabulary gain, ignoring pictures?
  2. Main effect of IV2 (Illustrations): Does adding pictures affect vocabulary gain, ignoring music?
  3. Interaction of IV1 × IV2: Does the effect of music depend on whether pictures are present?
📌 The Golden Rule of Interactions

Always look at the interaction first. If the interaction is statistically significant, main effects must be interpreted in light of the interaction — and are often misleading on their own. If the interaction is not significant, then main effects can be interpreted independently.

Interaction Plots

The best way to understand an interaction is through an interaction plot. Each line represents one level of one IV, and the x-axis shows the levels of the other IV. Non-parallel lines signal an interaction — the effect of one variable differs across levels of the other.

✅ No Interaction — Lines Are Parallel

Music adds the same amount of gain regardless of whether pictures are present. The effect of Music is consistent across both levels of Illustrations.

⚡ Interaction — Lines Cross or Diverge

Music helps when there are no pictures but hurts when pictures are present. The effect of Music changes direction depending on the level of Illustrations — a clear interaction.

12.4Example Study: Obarow (2004) with Gender

To illustrate the full power of factorial ANOVA, we extend Obarow's two-way design by adding a third IV: Gender (male/female). This creates a 2 (Music) × 2 (Illustrations) × 2 (Gender) factorial ANOVA. The logic of main effects and interactions is the same whether you have two or three IVs.

What Three-Way Partitioning Looks Like

With three IVs (Music, Pictures, Gender), the variance is partitioned into seven separate effects:

💡 Reading Order: Start from the Top Interaction

Always interpret the highest-order interaction first (here, the three-way). If it is significant, main effects are secondary — the main effects can only be understood in the context of the interaction. If the three-way is not significant, examine two-way interactions next, then main effects last.

12.5How Variance Is Partitioned

Just as in a one-way ANOVA, the total variance is split into explained and unexplained portions. A factorial ANOVA divides the explained variance further — one slice for each main effect and one for each interaction.

12.6Assumptions

  • 1
    Independence of observationsEach participant is in exactly one cell; scores in one group are unrelated to scores in others. Guaranteed by study design.
  • 2
    Interval-level DVThe dependent variable must be continuous. The Obarow vocabulary gain score is a count and treated as approximately interval-level.
  • 3
    Normality of residualsThe residuals (observed minus fitted values) should be approximately normally distributed. Check with a Q-Q plot in JASP's Assumption Checks menu.
  • 4
    Homogeneity of variancesVariance should be similar across all cells. Test with Levene's test. For the Obarow data: F(7, 56) = 1.88, p = .090 — not significant, assumption met.

How to Read This Table

  1. Start with the three-way interaction (Pictures × Music × Gender): F(1, 56) = 0.60, p = .443. Not significant. We can examine the two-way interactions next.

  2. Examine two-way interactions (Pictures × Music, Pictures × Gender, Music × Gender): None are statistically significant (p > .05). Interaction plots show lines that are not perfectly parallel, but the differences are within chance variation.

  3. Examine main effects: Only Gender is significant, F(1, 56) = 4.35, p = .042, ηp² = .072. Males gained more vocabulary on average than females, regardless of music or pictures condition.

  4. Note the overall model fit: R² = .148 (the model accounts for about 15% of the variance in gain scores). The adjusted R² = .042, reflecting the large number of predictors relative to the modest sample size.

📌 Type II Sum of Squares

JASP defaults to Type III SS. Always change this to Type II SS in the Model menu. Type II is preferred when your research question focuses on main effects and their interactions, and is more appropriate when group sizes are unequal. Type III gives different (and typically misleading) results when the design is unbalanced.

12.8Post-Hoc Tests

Post-hoc tests are needed when a significant effect involves an IV with more than two levels — because with three or more levels, a significant F-test alone does not tell you which groups differ.

SituationDo You Need Post-Hoc Tests?
Significant main effect, IV has 2 levelsNo — examine descriptive means; the direction is clear.
Significant main effect, IV has 3+ levelsYes — run post-hoc pairwise tests to find which groups differ.
Significant interactionSkip main-effect post-hocs. Use interaction-specific follow-up comparisons conditioned on each level of one variable.
No significant effectsTechnically, stop. If using CIs, you may still inspect comparisons of interest.

For the Obarow data, Gender is the only significant effect, and it has only two levels (male/female) — so we can read the direction directly from the descriptive statistics: males outperformed females. No post-hoc test is needed.

If a significant interaction had been found (e.g., Music × Gender), we would run conditional comparisons: compare males vs. females separately at each level of Music (with music / without music). JASP supports this through its Post Hoc Tests menu using "Conditional comparisons for interactions."

12.9Effect Sizes

Effect SizeUsed ForNotes
Partial η² (ηp²)Each F-test row in the ANOVA tableProportion of variance explained by that effect, controlling for all other effects. Reported by JASP directly. Slightly positively biased.
Partial ω² (ωp²)Each F-test row (preferred with small N)Corrects for the positive bias in ηp². Available in JASP. Recommended when sample size is small relative to the number of cells.
R²Whole modelTotal variance explained by all effects together. Not automatically shown in JASP's ANOVA output — obtain via Linear Regression module using the same IVs.
Cohen's dₛPairwise post-hoc comparisonsStandardized mean difference between two specific cells. Available in JASP's Post Hoc Tests menu.
💡 ηp² vs. ω²p for Small Samples

With small samples (like the Obarow cells, where some cells had as few as five participants), partial omega-squared is preferred because eta-squared overestimates the population effect size. In JASP, simply select ωp² from the Estimates of effect size dropdown instead of ηp².

12.10Writing Up Results

Model Reporting Paragraph — Two-Way (+ Gender) Factorial ANOVA

A 2 (Music: music vs. no music) × 2 (Illustrations: pictures vs. no pictures) × 2 (Gender: male vs. female) between-subjects factorial ANOVA was conducted on vocabulary gain scores from Obarow (2004). Levene's test indicated that the equal-variances assumption was met, F(7, 56) = 1.88, p = .090. The Q-Q plot of residuals did not reveal serious departures from normality.

The three-way interaction between Music, Illustrations, and Gender was not statistically significant, F(1, 56) = 0.60, p = .443, ηp² = .011. Nor were any of the two-way interactions significant: Music × Illustrations, F(1, 56) = 0.73, p = .398; Music × Gender, F(1, 56) = 2.42, p = .126; Illustrations × Gender, F(1, 56) = 0.12, p = .733. Among the main effects, only Gender was statistically significant, F(1, 56) = 4.35, p = .042, ηp² = .072 — a small-to-medium effect by Cohen's (1988) guidelines. Males (M = 1.53, SD = 1.76, n = 34) gained more vocabulary than females (M = 0.67, SD = 1.12, n = 30), regardless of music or illustrations condition. The model accounted for approximately 15% of the variance in gain scores (R² = .148), although the adjusted estimate was considerably lower (R² = .042), reflecting uncertainty due to the relatively small sample sizes in each cell.
