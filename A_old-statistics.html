The "Old" Statistics

Null Hypothesis Significance Testing (NHST)

The Old Statistics (this page) The New Statistics →

To the [researcher] who practices this religion, Statistics refers to the seeking out and interpretation of p-values. Like any good religion, it involves vague mysteries capable of contradictory and irrational interpretation…

— David S. Salsburg (1985, p. 220)

Most of the statistical results you will read in published second language research are reported in the framework of null hypothesis significance testing (NHST). Understanding how NHST works — and where it falls short — is essential both for reading existing research critically and for understanding why this book advocates a different approach.

A.1What Is NHST?

NHST is a formal framework for deciding whether to treat an observed result as real or as likely due to chance. At its heart, it involves setting up a null hypothesis — a statement that there is no effect, no difference, or no relationship — and then asking: how likely is it that we would see data like ours if the null hypothesis were true?

If that probability is small enough (traditionally below α = .05), we reject the null hypothesis and accept the alternative hypothesis — the claim that there is a difference or relationship.

📌 The Null and Alternative Hypothesis

The null hypothesis (H₀) proposes no effect: for example, "There is no difference in language learning anxiety between the class that receives pre-class massages and the class that does not."

The alternative hypothesis (H₁) proposes that an effect exists: "There is a difference in language learning anxiety between the two classes."

Notice that researchers test the null hypothesis, not their own research hypothesis. This is because it is logically easier to disprove something than to prove it — just as it is easier to prove that unicorns do not exist by finding none than to prove they do exist.

A.2How NHST Works

The NHST Process
1
State the null hypothesis (H₀) — assume there is no effect or relationship in the population.
2
Choose an alpha level (α) — the threshold for "acceptable" probability of a false positive. In SLA research, α = .05 is standard: you accept a 5% chance of incorrectly rejecting H₀.
3
Collect data and compute a test statistic — e.g., t, F, r, or χ². This number summarizes how far your data are from what H₀ predicts.
4
Find the critical value — the test statistic threshold beyond which only α% of results would fall if H₀ were true. Depends on the test and degrees of freedom (df).
5
Calculate the p-value — the probability of obtaining a test statistic this large or larger if H₀ were true. The computer does this automatically.
6
Make a binary decision — if p < α, reject H₀ and declare the result "statistically significant." If p ≥ α, fail to reject H₀.

Degrees of Freedom

Most statistical tests report degrees of freedom (df), which is a mathematical correction related to the number of participants or observations minus the number of estimated parameters. Larger df values generally mean more information in the data — and a critical value that is easier to reach. You will always see df reported alongside a test statistic, for example: t(38) = 2.05, or F(2, 39) = 9.05.

A.3What Is a p-Value?

The p-value is one of the most frequently reported — and most frequently misunderstood — numbers in social science research. Here is its precise meaning:

The correct interpretation of a p-value

"The probability of finding a [statistic] this large or larger if the null hypothesis were true is [p-value]."

Written formally, the p-value is P(Data | H₀) — the probability of the data, given the null hypothesis. It is not P(H₀ | Data) — the probability that the null hypothesis is true, given your data. These two probabilities are completely different things.

A useful analogy: the probability of being found dead, given you were hanged, P(Dead|Hanged), is very different from the probability of having been hanged, given you were found dead, P(Hanged|Dead). Confusing these is a fundamental logical error.

💡 What "Statistically Significant" Actually Means

Declaring a result "statistically significant" only means that, if the null hypothesis were true, data as extreme as yours would occur less than α = .05 (5%) of the time. It says nothing about whether the effect is large, practically important, or likely to replicate. The word "significant" in everyday English implies importance — in statistics, it means only that a threshold probability was crossed.

A.4Common Misinterpretations of p-Values

NHST has persisted partly because p-values feel like they answer questions researchers actually care about. But they answer a much narrower question than most people assume. Here are the most common misinterpretations:

  • ✗
    WRONG: "p = .03 means there is a 3% chance the null hypothesis is true."
    CORRECT: p = .03 means there is a 3% chance of finding data this extreme if H₀ were true. It says nothing about the probability that H₀ is true.
  • ✗
    WRONG: "p = .03 means there is a 97% chance the result is real / will replicate."
    CORRECT: The p-value makes no claim about replication. p-values are themselves highly variable across samples (see Section A.8 below).
  • ✗
    WRONG: "p < .05 means the effect is large or practically important."
    CORRECT: Statistical significance depends on sample size. With a very large sample, tiny and meaningless differences become "statistically significant." Only effect sizes tell you about practical importance.
  • ✗
    WRONG: "p > .05 means there is no effect."
    CORRECT: Failing to reject H₀ does not prove H₀ is true. The study may simply have lacked sufficient power (sample size) to detect a real effect. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
  • ✗
    WRONG: "p = .049 is a more important result than p = .051."
    CORRECT: The .05 cutoff is an arbitrary convention. A result at p = .051 is not meaningfully different from p = .049 — but under NHST, one is "significant" and one is not.

A.5Type I and Type II Errors

Because NHST makes binary decisions based on imperfect data, two types of error are always possible:

Outcome of study →
True situation ↓
Study finds: No effect
Study finds: Effect exists
True: No effect in population
✓ Correct decisionProbability = 1 − α
✗ Type I Error (false positive)Probability = α (typically .05)
Called being "overeager"
True: Effect exists in population
✗ Type II Error (false negative)Probability = β
Called being "overly cautious"
✓ Correct decision (Power)Probability = 1 − β

The fundamental tension: lowering α (e.g., from .05 to .01) reduces the chance of Type I errors but increases the chance of Type II errors. The two types of error are inversely related, and the only way to reduce both simultaneously is to increase the sample size.

A.6Statistical Power

Power is the probability of correctly detecting a real effect when one exists in the population. It equals 1 − β (one minus the probability of a Type II error). A study with low power is likely to miss real effects — it produces inconclusive results even when something true and interesting is happening.

FactorEffect on Power
Increasing sample size (N)Increases power — the most effective lever
Larger true effect sizeIncreases power — larger effects are easier to detect
Higher α (e.g., .10 instead of .05)Increases power but also increases Type I error risk
Better measurement reliabilityIncreases power by reducing noise
One-tailed vs. two-tailed testOne-tailed has more power, but requires a directional prediction
⚠ SLA Research and Low Power

SLA research has a well-documented problem with small sample sizes, and therefore low power. When a study does not find a "significant" result, the most likely explanation is often that the sample was too small to detect the true effect — not that no effect exists. Fewer, better-powered studies with larger samples would serve the field better than many underpowered ones.

A.7The Problem of Multiple Testing

Every time you run a statistical test at α = .05, you accept a 5% chance of a false positive. If you run ten tests on the same dataset, the familywise error rate rises to approximately 40% — meaning there is a roughly 40% chance that at least one of your ten results is a false positive.

Two approaches for controlling the familywise error rate are commonly used:

MethodHow It WorksLimitation
Bonferroni Correction Divide the desired α by the number of tests. For 4 tests at α = .05, use α = .05/4 = .0125 for each test. Very conservative when many tests are run; reduces power substantially
False Discovery Rate (FDR) Controls the proportion of rejected hypotheses that are false positives. More powerful than Bonferroni. Available in R (not yet in JASP). More complex to implement; not widely used in SLA yet

A.8The Problems with NHST

NHST has faced sustained criticism from statisticians and methodologists for decades. The following problems are well-documented in the literature and directly relevant to SLA research:

🚨 Problem 1: p-Values Are Highly Variable

If you repeated the same experiment many times, drawing different samples from the same population, the p-values you obtained would vary wildly. Cumming (2012) showed that if you get p = .05 in a two-tailed test, an 80% prediction interval for the p-value in a replication would range from .00 to .38 — and a 95% interval would range from .00 to .82. In other words, a "significant" result at p = .05 might just as easily have been .38 or .79 with a different sample.

🚨 Problem 2: Statistical Significance Is Driven by Sample Size

With a large enough sample, any difference — no matter how trivially small — will be "statistically significant." With a small sample, even a large and practically important effect may not reach significance. This makes the p-value an unreliable guide to whether a finding is meaningful.

🚨 Problem 3: The Binary Decision Is Arbitrary and Crude

The threshold of α = .05 is a convention, not a natural law. A result of p = .049 is declared "significant" and one of p = .051 is declared "non-significant" — yet the two results contain nearly identical information. This black-and-white decision framework ignores the continuous nature of evidence.

🚨 Problem 4: Publication Bias (The File Drawer Problem)

Studies that fail to find "significant" results are far less likely to be published. This means the published literature over-represents positive findings. When researchers obtain non-significant results, they tend to search for alternative explanations rather than concluding that their sample was simply too small. This distorts our understanding of what effects are real.

🚨 Problem 5: NHST Does Not Answer What Researchers Want to Know

Researchers want to know: Was there an effect? How large was it? How practically important is it? A p-value answers only a narrow question about the probability of the data under H₀ — it says nothing about effect size, practical importance, or the precision of estimates. This is the fundamental argument for the "new" statistics (see next page).
