Foundations · Part B

The "New" Statistics

Confidence Intervals, Effect Sizes & Estimation

← The Old Statistics The New Statistics (this page)

The main difference in the "new" statistics is an emphasis on the size of effects, measured through confidence intervals and effect sizes, over dichotomous "significant vs. non-significant" p-value decisions associated with NHST.

The "new" statistics — a term used with the authors' tongues firmly in their cheeks, since these ideas have been around for 60 years or more — represent a fundamental shift in how researchers think about and report results. Rather than asking "is this statistically significant?", the new approach asks: How large is the effect, and how precisely have we estimated it?

This chapter introduces the two core tools of the new statistics: confidence intervals (CIs) and effect sizes.

B.1Why a New Approach?

The continued practice of NHST has been labelled as crude, uninformative, arbitrary, and unreliable because of its reliance on sample size. It has been called "inefficient at best, misleading at worst." Every major methodological society that has issued statistical reporting guidelines has insisted that the emphasis should shift away from dichotomous p-value decisions and toward effect sizes and confidence intervals.

Major SLA journals now require effect size reporting as standard practice, including Language Learning, Studies in Second Language Acquisition, The Modern Language Journal, TESOL Quarterly, Foreign Language Annals, Language Testing, Second Language Research, and Language Learning & Technology. The APA Publication Manual states that complete reporting of effect sizes is a minimum expectation for all APA journals.

B.2The Right Questions

Faulkner et al. (2008) asked the authors of 193 published studies what they actually wanted to know when reading about a study's results. The answers were consistent: Was there an effect? How large was it? How practically important is it? A dichotomous NHST decision addresses only the first question, and imperfectly at that. The new statistics addresses all three.

Old Statistics Asks
"Is the p-value below .05?"
"Can we reject H₀?"
"Is the result statistically significant?"
New Statistics Asks
"Was there an effect, and how large was it?"
"How precisely have we estimated this effect?"
"How practically important is this for learners?"

B.3Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval (CI) sets plausible limits for the value of a parameter — such as a mean score, a mean difference, or a correlation coefficient. Rather than a single-point estimate, a CI gives a range of values within which the true population parameter is likely to fall.

The agreed interpretation of a 95% CI is that it is "one from the notionally infinite number of intervals we would obtain if the experiment were repeated indefinitely." If we have a 95% CI, we can say that we are 95% confident that our interval includes the true population value (µ).

Reading a Confidence Interval

CIs do the same work as p-values — and more. If a 95% CI for a mean difference does not contain zero, the result is statistically significant at α = .05. But the CI also tells you how large the effect might be, and how uncertain your estimate is.

Interpreting CIs for a Mean Difference — Does the Interval Cross Zero?
CI entirely negative
✓ Significant: Group 2 scored higher
CI crosses zero
✗ Not significant: Zero is plausible
CI entirely positive
✓ Significant: Group 1 scored higher
The CI also shows the width of uncertainty. A narrow CI = precise estimate. A wide CI = uncertain estimate — the true effect could be anywhere in a wide range.

What CIs Tell You That p-Values Cannot

❌ A p-value alone tells you:
  • Whether a threshold was crossed (p < .05 or not)
  • Nothing about effect size
  • Nothing about the precision of the estimate
  • Nothing about practical importance
✓ A CI tells you:
  • Whether there was a significant effect (does zero fall inside?)
  • The size of the effect in the original measurement scale
  • The precision of the estimate (width of the interval)
  • A range of plausible effect magnitudes

B.4Precision and the Margin of Error

The precision of a CI is defined as half the width of the CI — also called the margin of error (MOE). A narrow CI is more precise: it pinpoints the true parameter more closely. A wide CI is less precise: the true effect could be anywhere in a large range, and the estimate is therefore less useful.

📌 The Political Poll Analogy

When a political poll reports that "Candidate A leads by 7 points, with a margin of error of ±3 points," the margin of error is exactly the MOE in statistics. The lead could be as small as 4 or as large as 10 points — there is wiggle room. If the margin of error were ±0.1 points, the estimate would be much more precise and you could be more confident that the lead is real.

How to Increase Precision

  • Increase sample size — the most direct lever. Larger N produces narrower CIs.
  • Include repeated measures — testing the same participants more than once or averaging across multiple tests reduces noise.
  • Use measures with better reliability and validity — unreliable measurement adds noise that widens the CI.

B.5The Hidden Variability of p-Values

One of the most surprising findings from modern statistics is that p-values are themselves highly variable across samples. If you repeated the same experiment many times — drawing different samples from the same population — you would get very different p-values each time, even if the true effect remained constant.

Cumming (2012) calculated p-intervals, the prediction intervals for what p-value you would get in a replication. If your original experiment gave p = .05 in a two-tailed test, an 80% prediction interval for the replication p-value would range from .00 to .38 — meaning there is a 20% chance the replication p-value would exceed .38. A 95% p-interval would range from .00 to .82. In other words, a "significant" result could just as easily have been "non-significant" with a different sample from the same population.

⚠ The Illusion of Precision

Reporting a precise-looking p-value like p = .034 creates an illusion of precision. The true sampling variability of that value is enormous. In contrast, a CI — though it looks less precise because it gives a range rather than a single number — actually reflects the true uncertainty in the estimate more honestly. Reporting CIs is not trading precision for imprecision: it is revealing the variability that was always there but hidden behind a single misleading number.

B.6Effect Sizes

An effect size is a measure of how large or important a result is. Unlike p-values, which change with sample size, effect sizes do not change no matter how many participants there are. This makes them the most informative answer to what researchers actually want to know.

Effect sizes are commonly divided into three families:

d-Family
Group Difference Indexes

Measure the difference between two group means in standard deviation units. Cohen's d, Hedges' g. Used with t-tests and ANOVA post-hocs.

r-Family
Relationship Indexes

Measure how strongly two variables co-vary. Includes r, r², η², ω², partial η², partial ω². Used with correlations and ANOVA omnibus tests.

Risk Estimates
Unstandardized Indexes

Include odds ratios (OR) and hazard ratios (HR). Common in medical research and logistic regression. Considered unstandardized effect sizes.

B.7The d-Family: Group Difference Effect Sizes

The prototypical d-family measure is Cohen's d. It expresses how large the difference between two group means is in standard deviation units. If Cohen's d = 1.00, the two means differ by exactly one standard deviation. Values above 1.00 are possible.

📌 The Standardizer Choice Matters

There are several options for the standardizer (denominator) in Cohen's d:
A. The SD of one specific group (often the control group)
B. The pooled SD of the two groups being compared (most common; recommended when variances are equal)
C. The pooled SD of all groups in the study

Because the standardizer choice affects the resulting d value, you must always state which standardizer you used when reporting a d-family effect size. Cohen's dₛ (pooled SD) and Glass' Δ (SD of the control/reference group) are the most common choices in SLA.

💡 Calculating Effect Sizes

JASP reports effect sizes in the output for most analyses when you check the relevant box. An online calculator at www.psychometrica.de/effect_size allows you to calculate Cohen's dₛ, Hedges' gₛ, and their 95% CIs from means, SDs, and group sizes — and is especially useful for post-hoc comparisons from ANOVA output.

B.8The r-Family: Relationship Effect Sizes

The r-family measures how much two variables co-vary, or how much variance in one variable is explained by another. Unlike d-family measures, r-family measures are constrained between .00 and 1.00 (for squared versions), which many find easier to interpret.

StatisticUsed WithInterpretation
Pearson's rCorrelationDirection and strength of linear relationship (−1 to +1). r² = proportion of variance explained.
η² (eta-squared)ANOVA omnibus testProportion of total variance explained by the IV. Slightly positively biased.
ω² (omega-squared)ANOVA omnibus testLike η² but corrected for positive bias. Preferred, especially with small N.
Partial η² (η²p)Factorial ANOVAVariance explained by one effect, controlling for others. Reported by JASP automatically.
Partial ω² (ω²p)Factorial ANOVALike partial η² but less biased. Preferred over partial η² with small samples.
⚠ Effect Sizes Have CIs Too

Effect sizes are point estimates, just like mean scores — they come from a sample and are subject to sampling variability. Always report a 95% CI alongside every effect size. A wide CI for an effect size (e.g., d = 1.48 [0.61, 2.24]) tells you that the true effect could be anywhere in that range, and you should treat the point estimate with caution. This is especially important in SLA research, where small samples are common.

B.9Benchmarks for Interpreting Effect Sizes

Effect size benchmarks provide rough guidelines for judging whether an effect is small, medium, or large. Critically, SLA research has its own benchmarks developed by Plonsky and Oswald (2014), based on thousands of real SLA studies. These differ from Cohen's general benchmarks because SLA effects tend to be larger than those in experimental psychology.

📌 Benchmarks Are Not Laws

Effect size benchmarks are useful reference points, but context matters. Rosenthal (1990) noted that the correlation between taking aspirin and reduced heart attack risk was r = .03 — a tiny effect by any benchmark, yet medically important enough to recommend aspirin to millions of people. Always interpret effect sizes in light of the specific research question, the cost of interventions, and the field's standards.

B.10Reporting in Practice

The new statistics does not require abandoning p-values entirely — they are still useful in some contexts (e.g., omnibus ANOVA tests, which do not produce CIs). The practical recommendation is to report CIs and effect sizes alongside or instead of p-values wherever possible.

ContextRecommended Reporting
Mean scores (descriptives)Report M, SD, and 95% CI for the mean
Correlationr, 95% CI [lower, upper], N, r² — no p-value needed
Paired t-testMean difference, 95% CI, Cohen's dᵣₘ with 95% CI
Independent t-testMean difference, 95% CI, Hedges' gₛ with 95% CI
One-way ANOVA (omnibus)F, df, p, ω² with 95% CI (no CI for the omnibus)
Post-hoc comparisonsMean difference, 95% BCa CI, Hedges' gₛ with 95% CI

Effect sizes generallyAlways include the 95% CI for the effect size itself
📖 The Difference That p Cannot Show

Consider two fictional studies comparing teaching methods on a grammar cloze test. Study A has 10 participants and finds a mean difference of 15 points, p = .08. Study B has 1,000 participants and finds a mean difference of 0.5 points, p = .003.

Under NHST, Study B is "significant" and Study A is not. But Study A found a 15-point difference — three times the maximum score in some cloze tests — and was simply underpowered. Study B found a 0.5-point difference that is statistically significant but educationally meaningless. Effect sizes and CIs reveal this; a bare p-value hides it.
