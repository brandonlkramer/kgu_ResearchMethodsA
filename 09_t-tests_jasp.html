Running T-Tests in JASP

Step-by-step instructions with annotated screenshots

This page walks you through running both types of t-tests in JASP, step by step. Each numbered instruction is paired with an annotated screenshot showing exactly what to click. Red boxes in the screenshots highlight the key settings or output values to pay attention to.

📂 Sample Data

Independent samples: Use the Leow & Morgan-Short (2004) dataset (LeowMorganShort.csv).
Paired samples: Use the Kim (2013) dataset (Kim2013.csv).
Make sure variable types are set correctly before beginning — Scale for continuous variables, Nominal for group labels.

Part A · Independent Samples t-Test

Use this test when comparing two separate groups of different participants on the same measure. In this example: think-aloud vs. non-think-aloud groups on a receptive posttest.

9A.1Setting Up the Analysis

  1. 1

    In the top menu bar, click T-Tests.

  2. 2

    From the dropdown, select Independent Samples T-Test.

Fig 9A–1 Selecting the Independent Samples T-Test from the T-Tests menu
screenshots/fig9a-1.png
T-Tests menu → Independent Samples T-Test
→

Click T-Tests in the top ribbon, then choose Independent Samples T-Test from the dropdown. This opens the analysis dialog on the right side of the screen.

  1. 3

    Move your dependent variable (the continuous outcome — here, RecPostScore) into the Dependent Variables box by clicking the arrow button.

  2. 4

    Move your grouping variable (the categorical group label — here, Group) into the Grouping Variable box.

  3. 5

    Under Tests, check ☑ Welch. This is recommended over Student's t-test because it does not assume equal variances between groups.

  4. 6

    Under Additional Statistics, check ☑ Effect size. Select Glass' delta if your group variances are unequal (check the output later), or Cohen's d if they are equal. Also check ☑ Confidence interval for the effect size.

  5. 7

    Under Additional Statistics, check ☑ Descriptives to include means and SDs in the output.

  6. 8

    Under Assumption Checks, check ☑ Normality, ☑ Equality of variances, and ☑ Q-Q plot residuals.

  7. 9

    Under Plots, check ☑ Raincloud plots to visualize how scores are distributed in each group.

①

Top box (Dependent Variables): RecPostScore should appear here.

9A.2Output: Descriptive Statistics & Plots

The first section of the output shows summary statistics for each group and a raincloud plot visualizing the full distribution. Always examine these before interpreting the test result.

Fig 9A–3 Group Descriptives table
screenshots/fig9a-3.png
Group Descriptives table — N, Mean, SD for each group
①

Record the Mean and SD for each group — you will need these for your write-up. Here: Think-Aloud (M = 5.89, SD = 6.04, n = 38) vs. Non-Think-Aloud (M = 3.92, SD = 4.31, n = 39).

Notice the large difference in SDs (6.04 vs. 4.31). The variances (SD²) are roughly 36 vs. 18 — this signals heterogeneous variances, and confirms we should use Welch's test.

Fig 9A–4 Raincloud plot
screenshots/fig9a-4.png
Raincloud plot — distribution of scores for each group
→

The raincloud plot shows the raw data points (dots), a boxplot, and a density curve side by side. Check: Are the distributions roughly symmetric? Are there outliers? Is the spread similar between groups? These are visual checks on the normality and equal-variance assumptions.

9A.3Output: Assumption Checks & Test Results

Fig 9A–5 Assumption Checks — Normality (Shapiro-Wilk) and Equality of Variances
screenshots/fig9a-5.png
Assumption Checks: Shapiro-Wilk table + Brown-Forsythe table
①

Shapiro-Wilk normality test: If p < .05 for either group, the data depart from normality. Here both groups are significant (p < .001), so normality is violated. Consider bootstrapping.

Brown-Forsythe equality of variances: p = .005, meaning variances are not equal. This confirms we must use the Welch row in the main output — not the Student row.

Fig 9A–6 Independent Samples T-Test results table
screenshots/fig9a-6.png
T-test results: t, df, p, Mean Difference, 95% CI, Glass' delta
①

Read the Welch row (not Student). Key values to extract:

• t = −1.644 · df = 66.81 · p = .105

• 95% CI for the mean difference: [−4.37, 0.42] — this CI contains zero, so the result is not statistically significant.

• Glass' Δ = −0.33, 95% CI [−0.78, 0.13] — a small effect; CI also contains zero.

💡 How to read the 95% CI

If the confidence interval for the mean difference contains zero, the result is not statistically significant — zero is a plausible value for the true difference. If the interval is entirely positive or entirely negative, the result is significant. Always report the CI alongside (or instead of) the p-value.

9A.4Model Write-Up

Model Reporting Paragraph — Independent Samples t-Test

Data from Leow and Morgan-Short (2004) showed departures from normality and unequal variances between groups. An independent samples Welch's t-test found no evidence of a significant difference in receptive posttest scores between the think-aloud group (M = 5.89, SD = 6.04, n = 38) and the non-think-aloud group (M = 3.92, SD = 4.31, n = 39), t(66.81) = −1.64, p = .105, 95% CI [−4.37, 0.42]. Because the confidence interval contains zero, we cannot conclude that there is a real difference between groups. The effect size was Glass' Δ = −0.33 [−0.78, 0.13], which is considered small by Plonsky and Oswald's (2014) guidelines for SLA research.

Part B · Paired Samples t-Test

Use this test when the same participants are measured at two time points or on two related measures. In this example: the same learners are compared on a sarcasm comprehension pretest vs. posttest.

9B.1Setting Up the Analysis

  1. 1

    In the top menu bar, click T-Tests.

  2. 2

    From the dropdown, select Paired Samples T-Test.

  3. 3

    Click Pretest in the variable list, then click Posttest. Both will move together into the right panel as Pair 1. Repeat with Posttest and DelayedPost to create Pair 2.

  4. 4

    Under Tests, keep ☑ Student checked (the standard parametric paired test).

  5. 5

    Under Additional Statistics, check ☑ Effect size and select Cohen's dᵣₘ — this is recommended for repeated-measures designs. Also check ☑ Confidence interval.

  6. 6

    Under Assumption Checks, check ☑ Normality (this tests the difference scores, not the raw scores).

  7. 7

    Under Plots, check ☑ Raincloud plots.

Fig 9B–1 Paired Samples T-Test dialog with all settings selected
screenshots/fig9b-1.png
Dialog: variable pairs, Cohen's dᵣₘ, Normality, Raincloud
①

Pairs panel: Pair 1 shows Pretest ↔ Posttest, Pair 2 shows Posttest ↔ DelayedPost.

Effect size: Cohen's dᵣₘ is selected. This form of d is preferred for repeated-measures designs because it does not overestimate effects.

9B.2Output: Paired Samples Correlations

Before the main test result, JASP reports the correlation between each pair of scores. Because the same participants contribute scores at both times, we expect a positive correlation — the paired t-test relies on this.

Fig 9B–2 Paired Samples Correlations table
screenshots/fig9b-2.png
Paired Samples Correlations: r and p for each pair
→

Check that there is a correlation between the two variables. If r is near zero or negative (and not statistically significant), it may indicate that pairing is not useful. Here: Pair 1 r = −.34 (p = .371) — not significant, but the n is only 9, so low power is expected.

①

Key values for Pair 1 (Pretest – Posttest):

• Mean Difference = −9.11 · t(8) = −3.99 · p = .004

• 95% CI: [−14.37, −3.85] — the interval is entirely negative (does not cross zero), so the improvement from pretest to posttest is statistically significant.

For Pair 1: Cohen's drm = 2.18, 95% CI [0.21, 4.14]. The pretest-to-posttest improvement was more than twice the SD of the difference scores — a very large effect. However, the wide CI (0.21 to 4.14) reflects the small sample of only 9 participants.

The raincloud plot shows a clear upward shift from Pretest to Posttest. Include this figure in your write-up with a caption describing what the x- and y-axes represent and what the visual pattern tells us about the instructional effect.

⚠ Small Sample Note

With only N = 9 participants, effect size estimates and CIs will be wide and imprecise. The results are promising, but replication with a larger sample is needed before drawing strong conclusions.

9B.4Model Write-Up

Model Reporting Paragraph — Paired Samples t-Test

A paired samples t-test was conducted to examine whether sarcasm comprehension scores improved from the pretest to the immediate posttest following nine weeks of instruction. The pretest (M = 7.44, SD = 6.24) and posttest (M = 16.56, SD = 2.96) scores showed some departure from normality. Results indicated a statistically significant and large improvement, t(8) = −3.99, p = .004, 95% CI [−14.37, −3.85]. Because this confidence interval does not include zero and is entirely negative, we conclude that scores at posttest were significantly higher than at pretest. The effect size, Cohen's drm = 2.18, 95% CI [0.21, 4.14], indicates a very large effect, although the wide interval reflects the small sample size (N = 9) and uncertainty in the estimate. A comparison of the posttest and delayed posttest was also significant, t(8) = −3.44, p = .009, 95% CI [−5.20, −1.03], suggesting that gains were retained five weeks after instruction.
