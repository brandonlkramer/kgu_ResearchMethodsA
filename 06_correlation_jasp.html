Correlation in JASP

Step-by-step instructions with annotated screenshots

This page walks through the full correlation workflow in JASP using two example datasets — Flege et al. (1999) for a single correlation between Age of Arrival and English pronunciation, and DeKeyser (2000) for a scatterplot with grouped data. Click any dashed box to upload your screenshot. Red callouts show key values; blue callouts explain interpretation; yellow callouts flag common mistakes.

📂 Sample Data

Flege et al. (1999): FlegeYeniKomshianLiu.csv — variables include AOA (Age of Arrival, continuous Scale) and PronEng (English pronunciation rating 1–9, Scale). N = 240.
DeKeyser (2000): DeKeyser2000.csv — variables include Age (continuous), GJTScore (grammar test score), and Status (nominal group: arrived before/after age 15).

6A.1Step 1: Scatterplot First — Always

Before running any correlation, you must examine a scatterplot. In JASP, scatterplots are produced through the Descriptives module, not the Regression module where correlation lives.

  1. 1

    Load the Flege et al. data (FlegeYeniKomshianLiu.csv). Confirm AOA and PronEng are both set to Scale.

  2. 2

    Click the Descriptives module in the top menu.

  3. 3

    Move PronEng into the Variables box and AOA into the Variables box.

  4. 4

    Scroll down to the Basic Plots section. Check ☑ Scatter plots. JASP will produce a scatterplot for each pair of variables.

  5. 5

    Within the scatterplot options, ensure ☑ Regression line is selected. This adds the linear best-fit line to the plot.

Variables box: Both AOA and PronEng should appear here. JASP automatically generates a scatterplot for every pair of variables listed.

Fig 6–2Scatterplot output — AOA × Pronunciation Score
screenshots/fig6-2.png

→

What to look for: Is the trend roughly linear — does the regression line follow the data reasonably well? For the Flege data, the answer is yes: a clear, steep negative slope is visible, with data points clustering fairly tightly around the line.

Positive or negative? A line sloping down from left to right = negative correlation. As AOA increases (older), pronunciation scores decrease.

Outliers? Any points far from the regression line may be influential. The Flege data has a few — note their location but they do not disrupt the overall pattern here.

⚠ Check for Non-Linearity

If the Loess smoother (a flexible curved line) diverges substantially from the straight regression line, the relationship may be non-linear. A Pearson correlation summarizes a linear relationship — if the true relationship curves, the r value will underestimate the strength of the association. JASP's scatterplot includes a Loess option; always compare both lines.

6A.2Scatterplot with Groups

When you have a categorical variable that divides participants into groups, you can color-code the scatterplot to see whether the relationship differs across groups.

  1. 6

    Load the DeKeyser (2000) data. In Descriptives, move GJTScore and Age into the Variables box.

  2. 7

    Move Status (arrived before/after age 15) into the Split box directly below the Variables box. JASP will color-code the scatterplot points by group.

  3. 8

    Check ☑ Scatter plots and ☑ Regression line as before.

Fig 6–3Grouped scatterplot — DeKeyser (2000) data with Status as Split variable
screenshots/fig6-3.png
Scatterplot: Age on x-axis, GJT Score on y-axis, two colors for Status groups
→

Splitting by Status reveals two clusters: early arrivals (arrived before age 15) cluster in the top-left with higher scores at younger ages; late arrivals cluster in the bottom-right with lower scores at older ages. Both groups show negative trends, consistent with a critical period interpretation.

Seeing the data split by group can also warn you of Simpson's paradox — where a correlation within groups runs in the opposite direction from the overall correlation. Always check.

6A.3Setting Up the Correlation

Correlation in JASP lives under the Regression module — not under Descriptives or a dedicated Correlation module.

  1. 9

    Click the Regression module in the top menu bar.

  2. 10

    Select Correlation from the dropdown.

  3. 11

    Move AOA and PronEng into the Variables box. You can add as many variables as you want — JASP computes all pairwise correlations simultaneously.

Fig 6–4Regression module → Correlation dialog
screenshots/fig6-4.png
Correlation dialog: AOA and PronEng in Variables box
!

Common mistake: Looking for correlation under the wrong module. It is in Regression → Correlation, not in Descriptives or any dedicated Correlation section. If you can't find it, click the Regression module in the top ribbon first.

6A.4Options to Select

  1. 12

    Under Sample Correlation Coefficients, keep ☑ Pearson's r checked (the default). This is the standard parametric correlation.

  2. 13

    Under Additional Options, check ☑ Confidence intervals. Set the level to 95%. This is essential — always report CIs alongside r.

  3. 14

    Check ☑ Sample size — this adds N to the output table, which must always be reported.

  4. 15

    Uncheck ☒ Report significance and ☒ Flag significant correlations — these add p-values and asterisks, which are not recommended for correlations (see concept page Section 6.8). Removing them keeps the focus on effect size and CI.

Fig 6–5Correlation options — CI and Sample size checked, significance unflagged
screenshots/fig6-5.png
Options: Pearson's r checked; Confidence intervals 95% checked; Sample size checked; Report significance unchecked
①

Confidence intervals (95%): checked — gives the lower and upper bound of the CI for r.

Sample size: checked — adds N to the output, required for reporting.

Report significance / Flag significant correlations: both unchecked — removes p-values and asterisks from the table. The r value and its CI are more informative.

!

Spearman's ρ and Kendall's τ-b are also available as checkboxes. Leave these unchecked unless you have a specific reason to use a non-parametric test. For most SLA research with continuous variables, Pearson's r is appropriate.

6A.5Reading the Output

JASP produces a correlation table with one row per variable pair. The key columns are the correlation coefficient, the CI bounds, and the sample size.

Fig 6–6Correlation output table — AOA × PronEng
screenshots/fig6-6.png
Correlation table: Variables | r | 95% CI Lower | 95% CI Upper | N
①

Read the key values from the output row for AOA – PronEng:

• r = −.86 — a large negative correlation

• 95% CI = [−.89, −.82] — a narrow interval because N is large; zero is far outside

• N = 240 — report this alongside r

Calculate r² yourself: (−.86)² = .74 — AOA explains 74% of the variance in pronunciation ratings.

→

Interpreting the CI: Because the CI [−.89, −.82] does not contain zero, and is entirely on the negative side, we can conclude the true population correlation is negative and large. If the CI had contained zero (e.g., [−.20, .05]), we could not rule out the possibility of no relationship.

6A.6Running Multiple Correlations at Once

When you have several variables and want all pairwise correlations simultaneously, simply add more variables to the Variables box. JASP will automatically produce a full correlation matrix.

  1. 16

    In the Correlation dialog, move additional variables into the Variables box alongside AOA and PronEng. For the Flege dataset, you might add LOR (length of residence) or other continuous measures.

  2. 17

    The output table will expand to show a row for every pair of variables. Each cell contains r, 95% CI, and N.

Fig 6–7Multiple correlations — expanded output table with several variable pairs
screenshots/fig6-7.png
Correlation matrix table: multiple variables, r + CI + N for every pair
→

The matrix is symmetric — the correlation between Variable A and Variable B is the same as between B and A. JASP displays the lower triangle by default. For reporting, you can present this as a table in your paper, listing only the values in one triangle to avoid redundancy.

Look across the rows to compare which variable is most strongly related to your outcome of interest. Wide CIs indicate pairs with small N or weak relationships — treat those estimates with caution.

6A.7Scatterplot Matrix

When running multiple correlations, a scatterplot matrix (sometimes called a pairs plot or splom) shows all pairwise scatterplots simultaneously in a grid — one plot per pair. This is an efficient way to visually check linearity and outliers for every combination at once.

  1. 18

    In the Correlation dialog, scroll down to the Plots section.

  2. 19

    Check ☑ Scatter plots. JASP will produce a grid of scatterplots, one for each pair of variables in your Variables box.

  3. 20

    Also check ☑ Densities for variables to add distribution curves along the diagonal of the matrix.

Fig 6–8Scatterplot matrix — all pairwise plots in a grid
screenshots/fig6-8.png
Scatterplot matrix: 3×3 or 4×4 grid of paired scatterplots with regression lines and density curves on diagonal
→

Scan the matrix quickly: panels where points cluster tightly along a line indicate strong correlations; scattered clouds indicate weak ones. The diagonal shows the distribution (histogram or density curve) of each variable individually — useful for checking normality.

Any panel that shows a curved, U-shaped, or fan-shaped pattern signals that the linearity or homoscedasticity assumption may be violated for that pair.

6A.8Model Write-Up

💡 What to include in every correlation report

① Name of the test (Pearson's r)  ·  ② The r value  ·  ③ 95% CI for r  ·  ④ Sample size N  ·  ⑤ r² (calculated from r)  ·  ⑥ Effect size interpretation using Plonsky & Oswald (2014) benchmarks for SLA. No p-value is necessary.

Model Reporting Paragraph — Single Correlation

To examine the relationship between age of arrival (AOA) in the USA and English pronunciation ability, a Pearson correlation was conducted on the Flege et al. (1999) dataset (N = 240). A scatterplot with regression and Loess lines confirmed an approximately linear negative relationship with no unusual outliers. The correlation was large and negative: r = −.86, 95% CI [−.89, −.82], r² = .74. This indicates that AOA accounts for approximately 74% of the variance in English pronunciation ratings. The narrow CI reflects the precision afforded by the large sample — we can be confident the true population correlation falls between r = −.82 and r = −.89, a consistently large effect by Plonsky and Oswald's (2014) SLA benchmarks. Learners who arrived in the USA at younger ages consistently received higher (more native-like) pronunciation ratings from native English speaker judges.

Model Reporting Paragraph — Multiple Correlations

Pearson correlations were examined among three variables in the Larson-Hall (2008) dataset (N = 200): scores on a grammaticality judgment test (GJT), total hours of English study, and language aptitude scores. A scatterplot matrix was inspected first; relationships appeared roughly linear and no influential outliers were identified. Hours of study showed a small positive relationship with GJT scores, r = .18, 95% CI [.05, .32], r² = .03 — English study time accounts for only approximately 3% of the variance in grammar scores, a small effect. The relationship between aptitude and GJT performance was negligible, r = .08, 95% CI [−.06, .22], r² < .01. Because the CI for the aptitude correlation spans zero, we cannot conclude there is a meaningful relationship between these two variables in this sample.
